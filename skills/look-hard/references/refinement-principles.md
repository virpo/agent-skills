# Refinement Principles

## Core standard

An artifact is good when its purpose, state, hierarchy, and next action are immediately legible—and when its details reinforce a specific character rather than generic polish.

## Inspect reality

- Judge the rendered page, exported deck, real device, final-size media, or physical object.
- Source code explains causes; it does not prove appearance or behavior.
- Verify that screenshots and files show the intended artifact and current revision.
- Preserve comparable before and after conditions: viewport, content, state, lighting, zoom, crop, or device.

## Start with the job

- Identify what the artifact must make someone understand, feel, or do.
- Make the primary state and next action unmistakable within seconds.
- Treat waiting, empty, error, selected, disabled, success, and quota states as real product surfaces.
- Prefer one clear lane over several plausible branches.

## Use four finding buckets

### Broken

Clipping, overlap, incorrect state, unreadable contrast, failed action, missing asset, bad crop, wrong dimensions, inaccessible control, stale facts, or behavior that violates the contract.

### Confusing

Competing primary actions, hidden state, unclear selection, ambiguous wording, weak grouping, unexpected movement, or a flow that forces interpretation.

### Generic or emotionally flat

Default-looking layouts, repeated cards, wrappers within wrappers, decoration without a point, generic copy, inconsistent visual metaphors, or a technically correct artifact with no memorable idea.

### Unfinished

Placeholder copy, uneven spacing, accidental alignment, inconsistent states, missing responsive behavior, abrupt transitions, unreviewed pages, or a handoff without proof.

## Rank by leverage

Prioritize in this order:

1. Correctness, access, safety, and legibility.
2. State and hierarchy.
3. Flow and next action.
4. Density, spacing, alignment, and rhythm.
5. Character, delight, and finish.

Do not spend the pass polishing a button while the wrong state or product promise dominates the surface.

## Subtract before decorating

- Remove duplicate labels, redundant explanations, competing actions, unnecessary borders, and nested containers.
- Let one strong visual, screenshot, object, line, or interaction own the moment.
- Add ornament only when it improves understanding, character, or useful feedback.
- Avoid generic AI tells: decorative gradients, glass by default, arbitrary glow, fake metrics, stock robots, and repeated rounded cards.

## Sweat spatial details

- Inspect actual usable width, especially on mobile.
- Check alignment by eye as well as by coordinates; optical centering matters.
- Use spacing to communicate grouping and sequence.
- Keep density intentional: compact is not cramped, sparse is not empty.
- Preserve readable contrast in dark mode and low-contrast states.
- Make touch targets and controls recognizable, not merely decorative.

## Write like a person

- Use sharp, short, concrete wording.
- Each sentence adds information.
- Prefer obvious language over slang, idioms, corporate mush, or clever ambiguity.
- Assume some readers are not native English speakers.
- Explain privileged, destructive, slow, or irreversible steps before they happen.
- If a line would sound unnatural aloud, rewrite it.

## Choose character deliberately

- A little quirky, playful, funky, or surprising is good when it fits the artifact.
- Readability wins over cleverness.
- Strong direction beats a safe average of several styles.
- A useful surprise should reveal understanding or remove work: “it already handled that,” not bolted-on gamification.
- Preserve distinctive decisions that already work instead of redesigning everything to show activity.

## Inspect the whole experience

- Desktop approval does not imply mobile approval.
- A ready state does not prove loading, error, empty, disabled, or selected states.
- A PDF does not prove animation timing; a source deck does not prove export layout.
- Automated tests do not prove visual quality; screenshots do not prove interaction.
- A render or CAD fit does not prove a physical recipe until the object is measured or tested.

## Finish with evidence

Compare before and after directly. Name regressions and remaining risks. If the last necessary proof belongs to a human, device, printer, store, production system, or inaccessible account, say exactly what remains and who can perform it.
